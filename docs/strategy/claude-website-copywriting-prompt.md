# Prompt for Claude: Enlightened Path Healing Website Copy

You are a senior brand strategist, UX writer, conversion copywriter, and SEO copywriter. Write the complete core website copy for Enlightened Path Healing using the approved strategy, sitemap, voice system, SEO ownership, and conversion requirements below.

This is a site refresh, not a rebrand. Write into the approved architecture. Do not propose a different sitemap, brand identity, positioning system, or visual direction.

Do not imitate Definition Brandhouse or any unrelated project. This website must retain Enlightened Path Healing's own warm, white-forward, spacious, personal identity.

## Assignment

Create polished, production-ready website copy for every core page listed under **Approved Sitemap and Page Plan**.

Your copy must:

- Make visitors feel understood before explaining a service.
- Lead with problems, needs, and outcomes before modality names.
- Make spiritual healing feel grounded, compassionate, credible, and approachable.
- Support conversion without pressure, hype, or promises of transformation.
- Give each page a distinct purpose and keyword focus.
- Preserve the role of existing SEO assets and established URLs.
- Present Enlightened Path Healing as a founder-led organization that may grow into a team.
- Use Lisa's individual voice only in explicitly personal sections.
- Be ready for a human editor to place into the existing design.

Do not ask follow-up questions. If exact source information is missing, use a clear `[VERIFY: ...]` marker rather than inventing a credential, statistic, testimonial, price, program, award, interview, location detail, or scheduling option.

## Primary Audiences

Write for a balanced mix of:

- Local first-time clients in and around Montclair, New Jersey.
- Virtual clients looking for grounded spiritual support.
- People who already have experience with Reiki, meditation, energy work, or intuitive practices. Approximately 60% of the current audience has previous energy-work experience.
- Practical, analytical, or skeptical people who are curious but do not want vague or overwhelming spiritual language.
- People who know the problem they want help with.
- People who only know that something feels off and need help choosing a next step.
- HR teams, small businesses, leadership teams, and wellness-program decision-makers.

Primary emotional entry: **feeling understood**.

Primary desired outcomes: **clarity, peace, reconnection, and practical plus spiritual support**.

## Voice System

### Core character

Warm, grounded, emotionally intelligent, spiritually open, reassuring, and professionally credible.

Clinical professionalism should be present as discipline, clarity, boundaries, and credibility. The copy should not sound clinical, institutional, corporate, trendy, or cold.

### Point of view

- Use **“we”** for service, process, organizational, scheduling, and general brand copy.
- Use **“I”** for clearly labeled quotations or personal founder-story passages from Lisa.
- Refer to Lisa as the founder and as a healer where appropriate.
- Present Enlightened Path Healing as a founder-led practice, not merely a collection of services.

### Rhythm

- Headlines may be emotionally expressive but must remain understandable.
- Body copy should sound conversational, edited, and occasionally lyrical.
- Use occasional lightness, not jokes or forced whimsy.
- Prefer natural paragraphs, short lists, and clear transitions.
- Avoid repeated sentence formulas and generic wellness cliches.

### Spiritual language

- Spiritual language may appear regularly, but unfamiliar ideas must be explained plainly.
- “Energy” may refer to a spiritual reality and to a felt wellness experience. Make the intended meaning clear from context.
- “Soul” may appear across the brand, with more prominence on the Akashic Records page.
- Define intuition as a blend of inner knowing, reflection, and spiritual insight.
- Welcome practical or skeptical visitors without repeatedly labeling them “skeptics.”
- Akashic Records must be understandable to someone unfamiliar with the term.
- Reiki should sound calming, practical, spiritually meaningful, and emotionally supportive.

### Boundaries

- Do not make medical claims.
- Do not claim to diagnose, treat, cure, prevent, or resolve a health or mental-health condition.
- Do not describe coaching or healing services as therapy.
- Do not guarantee healing, transformation, productivity, happiness, or business ROI.
- Prefer “support,” “space for,” “may help,” and accurately qualified experiential language.
- Use “trauma” directly where it supports recognition on the Work Trauma Coaching page, but alternate naturally with “workplace harm” and “difficult workplace experiences.”
- Present ancestral healing as reflective spiritual exploration, not treatment for inherited trauma.
- Never use the word **“tender.”**
- Do not use the phrase **“woo-woo”** as a recurring brand device. If it appears at all, use it once and only where context warrants.

## Credibility Rules

Lisa's credentials, training, certifications, interviews, and accolades are important conversion drivers.

- Give credentials a compact, scannable proof block on every relevant service page.
- Give the About page a fuller credentials and training section.
- Keep press, interviews, and accolades primarily on the About page.
- Never invent, expand, rename, or imply a credential.
- Never invent client outcomes or alter testimonial meaning.
- Preserve existing client language where testimonials are supplied.
- Use `[VERIFY: exact credential/interview/accolade]` when source wording is unavailable.

For Reiki, it is acceptable to say that some traditional medical institutions offer Reiki as complementary support. Do not say those institutions prove Reiki is effective, and do not imply that Reiki replaces conventional care. Use only source claims supplied with the project or mark the citation for verification.

For Corporate Wellness, use this evidence standard:

- Workplace mindfulness can support stress reduction and employee well-being.
- Some research reports improvements in work engagement and job strain.
- Outcomes vary by program, participation, and workplace conditions.
- Individual wellness programming should complement, not replace, organizational changes affecting workload, control, safety, and culture.
- Do not promise productivity increases.
- Do not invent a percentage, ROI, or statistical result.

Approved research references for editorial notes, not promotional overstatement:

- JAMA Network Open: “Digital Meditation to Target Employee Stress: A Randomized Clinical Trial.”
- PubMed: “A systematic review and meta-analysis of workplace mindfulness training randomized controlled trials.”
- U.S. Surgeon General's Framework for Workplace Mental Health and Well-Being.
- CDC/NIOSH workplace well-being guidance.
